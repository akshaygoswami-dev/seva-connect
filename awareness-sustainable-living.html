Sustainable Living: Small Changes, Lasting Impact

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

The average Indian generates over 0.12 kg of solid waste per day, and collectively the nation produces more than 62 million tonnes of waste annually — a figure growing at 4% every year. Sustainable living is not about grand sacrifices or expensive lifestyles; it is about making conscious, everyday choices that reduce our environmental footprint and preserve resources for future generations.

The Myth That Sustainability Is Expensive

One of the most persistent barriers to sustainable living is the misconception that it requires wealth. The reality is precisely the opposite. Reducing electricity consumption lowers your bills. Carrying a reusable bag eliminates repeat purchases of plastic ones. Growing kitchen herbs in small pots cuts grocery spending. In the Barak Valley — where traditional practices like using banana leaves as plates, composting organic waste, and rain-fed agriculture already exist — sustainability is not a foreign concept. It is a return to wisdom our grandparents practised naturally.

Yet modernisation has introduced new challenges. Single-use plastic consumption in Silchar's markets has surged, open waste burning pollutes neighbourhoods, and rising energy demand from air conditioning and appliances strains both household budgets and the power grid. Bridging traditional wisdom with modern awareness is the key.

Key Facts

  • If every Indian household switched to LED bulbs, the country could save over 100 billion kWh of electricity annually.
  • Food waste accounts for 8–10% of global greenhouse gas emissions — more than the aviation industry.
  • A single reusable cloth bag replaces approximately 700 disposable plastic bags over its lifetime.
  • India's per capita carbon footprint is 1.9 tonnes CO₂, but it is rising rapidly with urbanisation.

What You Can Do

  • Reduce before you recycle: The most effective waste strategy is not recycling — it is reducing consumption in the first place. Ask yourself before every purchase: do I truly need this?
  • Segregate waste at home: Separate wet (biodegradable) and dry (recyclable) waste. Composting kitchen scraps can cut your household waste output by up to 40%.
  • Choose energy efficiency: Switch to LED lighting, unplug devices when not in use, and consider 5-star rated appliances when replacing old ones. These changes pay for themselves within months.
  • Eat locally and seasonally: Food transported over long distances carries a heavy carbon footprint. The Barak Valley's abundant local produce — rice, citrus fruits, vegetables — is both fresher and greener.
  • Spread the practice: Sustainability scales through communities. Share tips with neighbours, organise clean-up drives, and encourage local shopkeepers to offer paper or jute alternatives to plastic bags.
