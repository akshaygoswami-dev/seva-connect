Child Protection: Safeguarding Our Future

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Every child deserves to grow up in an environment free from abuse, exploitation, and neglect. Yet millions of children across India face physical violence, emotional harm, child labor, and online exploitation every day. Protecting children is not only a moral imperative — it is a legal obligation under some of the strongest child protection laws in the world.

Understanding the Threat

Child abuse takes many forms: physical violence, sexual exploitation, emotional abuse, neglect, and forced labor. According to the National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB), India recorded over 1.6 lakh crimes against children in 2022, with a significant portion going unreported due to stigma and fear. The Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act, 2012, provides a comprehensive legal framework specifically designed to protect children under 18 from sexual assault, harassment, and pornography — with stringent penalties for offenders and mandatory reporting obligations for citizens.

In the Barak Valley, children from economically disadvantaged families are especially vulnerable. Poverty-driven child labor persists in tea stalls, brick kilns, and domestic settings across Silchar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi. The rapid spread of smartphones has also exposed children to online predators, cyberbullying, and inappropriate content — often without parental awareness or supervision.

Key Facts

  • The POCSO Act mandates reporting of child sexual abuse — failure to report is itself a punishable offence.
  • India's Child Labour (Prohibition & Regulation) Amendment Act, 2016 bans employment of children under 14 in all occupations.
  • Over 50% of children who experience abuse know their abuser personally — most cases occur within families or trusted circles.
  • CHILDLINE 1098 is India's 24/7 toll-free helpline for children in distress, operational across all districts of Assam.

Recognizing the Signs

Warning signs of child abuse include unexplained injuries, sudden behavioral changes, withdrawal from friends or activities, fear of specific adults, age-inappropriate sexual knowledge, declining academic performance, and frequent absences from school. A child who suddenly becomes aggressive, anxious, or excessively secretive may be silently calling for help. In the Barak Valley, cultural hesitancy to discuss abuse openly often allows perpetrators to continue unchecked for years.

What You Can Do

  • Learn the law: Familiarize yourself with the POCSO Act and the Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act. Understanding the legal framework empowers you to act decisively when a child is in danger.
  • Report immediately: If you suspect abuse, call CHILDLINE 1098 or contact your nearest police station. Under POCSO, any person who has knowledge of an offence is legally required to report it.
  • Monitor online activity: Ensure children's internet usage is age-appropriate. Use parental controls and have open, non-judgmental conversations about online safety.
  • Educate children: Teach children about safe and unsafe touch using age-appropriate language. Empower them to speak up and assure them that reporting abuse is never their fault.
  • Support vulnerable families: Poverty is a root driver of child labor and exploitation. Contributing to education funds, mid-day meal programs, and vocational training for parents can break the cycle.
