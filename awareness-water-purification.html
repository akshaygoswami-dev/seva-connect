Water Purification: Clean Water for Every Home

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Waterborne diseases kill over 500 children under five every single day worldwide — most of these deaths are entirely preventable with access to clean drinking water. In India, diarrhoeal disease remains the third leading cause of death among children, claiming nearly 100,000 young lives annually. The Jal Jeevan Mission aims to provide piped tap water to every rural household by 2026, yet millions of families still rely on untreated water from rivers, ponds, tubewells, and open wells that may harbour deadly pathogens.

Affordable Purification Methods

Clean water does not require expensive technology. Boiling remains the most reliable and universally accessible method — bringing water to a rolling boil for one minute kills virtually all bacteria, viruses, and parasites. Chlorination using sodium hypochlorite solution (available as household bleach at 4–6% concentration) can disinfect large volumes affordably; just two drops per litre, followed by a 30-minute wait, provides safe drinking water for an entire family.

For sustained household use, ceramic candle filters offer an excellent low-cost solution. These gravity-fed filters remove bacteria and sediment without electricity or running water, making them ideal for rural homes. More advanced UV purification systems use ultraviolet light to inactivate microorganisms and are increasingly affordable for semi-urban households. Each method has trade-offs in cost, convenience, and effectiveness — but any one of them is vastly better than drinking untreated water.

Key Facts

  • Approximately 7.6 crore (76 million) Indian households still lack access to safe piped drinking water at their premises.
  • Waterborne diseases like cholera, typhoid, and hepatitis A account for an estimated 37.7 million cases in India annually.
  • A ceramic candle filter costs as little as ₹500–₹1,500 and can serve a family for 1–2 years with proper maintenance.
  • Assam has among the highest rates of diarrhoeal illness in India, with flood-prone districts especially affected during monsoon months.

Barak Valley's Water Quality Challenge

In Barak Valley, annual flooding of the Barak, Kushiyara, and Sonai rivers contaminates shallow tubewells and open wells with floodwater carrying sewage, agricultural runoff, and animal waste. Post-flood, the incidence of acute diarrhoea, cholera, and typhoid spikes dramatically across Cachar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi districts. Even during dry months, iron and arsenic contamination of groundwater — a problem documented across Assam's alluvial plains — renders many tubewells unsafe for direct consumption without treatment.

What You Can Do

  • Always treat your water: Whether you boil, chlorinate, or filter, never drink water directly from a tubewell, river, or pond without purification — especially during and after floods.
  • Maintain your filter: Clean ceramic candles monthly by gently scrubbing with a soft brush under running water. Replace candles every 12–18 months or when flow rate drops significantly.
  • Store water safely: Use narrow-mouthed containers with lids. Never dip hands or used cups into stored drinking water. Clean storage vessels with soap and water weekly.
  • Get your water tested: Government PHE (Public Health Engineering) labs offer free or low-cost water testing. Request a test annually, especially if you use a tubewell or open well.
  • Advocate for connections: If your household lacks a Jal Jeevan Mission tap connection, apply through your Gram Panchayat or ward office. The scheme provides functional household tap connections (FHTC) free of cost to eligible families.
