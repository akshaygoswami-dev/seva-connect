Waste Dumping: The Environmental Crisis Next Door

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

India generates over 160,000 tonnes of municipal solid waste every day, yet only about 75–80% is collected and less than 30% is processed scientifically. The rest ends up in open dumps, drainage channels, riverbanks, and vacant lots — creating toxic breeding grounds for disease, contaminating soil and groundwater, and releasing methane and other greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. For communities in Barak Valley, unregulated waste dumping is not an abstract environmental issue — it is an immediate public health emergency.

The Hidden Dangers of Open Dumping

Open waste dumps attract disease vectors — flies, mosquitoes, rats, and stray animals — that spread infections including dengue, leptospirosis, and gastroenteritis. Decomposing organic waste releases leachate, a toxic liquid that seeps into the soil and contaminates groundwater wells that thousands of families depend on for drinking water. Burning of garbage at dump sites releases fine particulate matter (PM2.5), dioxins, and furans — carcinogenic compounds that cause respiratory disease, especially in children and the elderly living nearby.

Biomedical waste from clinics and nursing homes poses an even graver risk. Used syringes, soiled dressings, expired medicines, and pathological waste are frequently mixed with regular municipal waste in smaller towns. In Silchar, despite the Biomedical Waste Management Rules of 2016, enforcement remains inconsistent. Ragpickers — often children — are directly exposed to infectious materials without any protective equipment.

Key Facts

  • India's 3,159 urban local bodies collectively dump over 35,000 tonnes of waste into open sites daily without processing.
  • The Barak River receives untreated sewage and solid waste runoff from multiple towns, degrading water quality for downstream communities.
  • Methane from decomposing waste is 80 times more potent than CO₂ as a greenhouse gas over a 20-year period.
  • Only 22% of Indian cities have functional material recovery facilities for waste segregation and recycling.

Silchar's Waste Challenge

Silchar town generates approximately 120–140 tonnes of solid waste daily, but the existing collection infrastructure — open trucks, limited route coverage, and a single overburdened dumpsite at Meherpur — struggles to handle even half that volume. Residential colonies near the Barak riverfront frequently witness waste being dumped directly into the water, particularly during monsoon months when drainage systems overflow. The lack of a sanitary landfill or composting facility means that all collected waste ends up in open piles, compounding the environmental damage year after year.

What You Can Do

  • Segregate at source: Separate your household waste into wet (biodegradable), dry (recyclable), and hazardous (batteries, medicines, e-waste) categories before handing it to collectors. This single step can transform waste management outcomes.
  • Compost kitchen waste: Nearly 50% of household waste is organic. A simple backyard or balcony compost bin converts food scraps into nutrient-rich manure, reducing landfill burden significantly.
  • Refuse single-use plastics: Carry reusable bags, bottles, and containers. Avoid plastic-wrapped products where alternatives exist. Every piece of plastic refused is one less item in our rivers.
  • Join cleanup drives: Participate in Seva Connect's community cleanup campaigns along riverbanks, market areas, and public spaces. Collective action creates visible change and builds civic pride.
  • Hold authorities accountable: Use the Swachh Bharat app or file written complaints with your ward councillor when you observe illegal dumping. Civic pressure drives systemic change.

Take Action

Our rivers, our neighbourhoods, our health — all depend on how we manage waste. Join Seva Connect's environmental campaigns and help build a cleaner, safer Barak Valley for everyone.
