Youth Mental Health: Supporting Our Students

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

India is home to the largest youth population on earth, yet mental health remains one of the most neglected and stigmatised areas of healthcare. According to UNICEF's State of the World's Children report, at least one in seven Indian adolescents aged 10–19 lives with a diagnosed mental health condition. Suicide is now the leading cause of death among Indians aged 15–29, with over 13,000 student suicides recorded in 2022 alone — a number that represents only the cases officially reported. Behind each statistic is a young person who needed help and didn't receive it.

The Pressure Cooker of Academic Stress

India's hyper-competitive examination culture places extraordinary pressure on students. Board exams, entrance tests for engineering (JEE), medical (NEET), and civil services create a years-long gauntlet of high-stakes evaluation where a student's entire future appears to hinge on a single score. Coaching centres — particularly prevalent in Assam where students prepare for national-level competitive exams — often enforce gruelling 12–16 hour study schedules, leaving no time for recreation, friendships, or rest. When results don't meet expectations, students frequently experience devastating shame, compounded by family and social pressure.

Social media adds another layer of psychological burden. Constant exposure to curated highlight reels of peers' achievements fuels comparison anxiety, fear of missing out (FOMO), and feelings of inadequacy. Cyberbullying — which affects nearly 37% of Indian teenagers according to a 2023 CRY survey — can push vulnerable young people toward self-harm and isolation, particularly in the absence of trusted adults to confide in.

Key Facts

  • India has only 0.75 psychiatrists and 0.07 psychologists per 100,000 population — among the lowest ratios globally.
  • The NIMHANS helpline (080-46110007) and iCall (9152987821) provide free, confidential mental health support in multiple languages.
  • Over 50% of mental health conditions begin before age 14, but most go undiagnosed and untreated for years.
  • Assam recorded 4,410 suicide deaths in 2022, with the highest rates among the 18–30 age group — underscoring the urgency of youth-focused intervention.

Recognising the Warning Signs

Depression and anxiety in teenagers often manifest differently than in adults. Rather than expressing sadness verbally, young people may show persistent irritability, sudden withdrawal from friends and activities they once enjoyed, a dramatic drop in academic performance, changes in appetite or sleep patterns, unexplained physical complaints (headaches, stomachaches), or expressions of hopelessness about the future. In Barak Valley, where open discussion of mental health remains taboo in most families, these signs frequently go unnoticed or are dismissed as laziness, rebellion, or attention-seeking behaviour.

What You Can Do

  • Start the conversation: Ask young people in your life — your children, students, younger siblings — how they are feeling. Create a safe, judgement-free space where they know they can speak honestly without fear of punishment or dismissal.
  • Decouple worth from marks: Praise effort, curiosity, and character rather than exclusively celebrating exam results. Remind students that one test score does not define their entire life or potential.
  • Limit screen time constructively: Encourage digital boundaries — no phones at mealtimes, no social media after 9 PM. Model healthy habits yourself; children learn by watching adults.
  • Learn the helpline numbers: Save NIMHANS (080-46110007), Vandrevala Foundation (1860-2662-345), and iCall (9152987821) in your phone. Share them with students, teachers, and parents. A timely call can save a life.
  • Advocate for school counsellors: Urge school administrations and parent-teacher associations in Silchar and surrounding areas to appoint trained counsellors. Mental health support should be as normal as having a school nurse.
