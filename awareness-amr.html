Antimicrobial Resistance: The Silent Pandemic

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Antimicrobial resistance (AMR) occurs when bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites evolve to survive the drugs designed to kill them. The World Health Organization has declared AMR one of the top ten global public health threats facing humanity. If left unchecked, routine infections and minor injuries could once again become fatal — reversing a century of medical progress.

Why AMR Is a Crisis

Every time antibiotics are used incorrectly — whether through incomplete courses, self-medication without a prescription, or unnecessary use in livestock farming — bacteria that survive the treatment pass their resistance genes to the next generation. Over time, entire strains become immune to every available drug. These are the "superbugs" that modern medicine has no answer for.

In India alone, drug-resistant infections cause an estimated 58,000 neonatal deaths every year. The Barak Valley is particularly vulnerable: limited access to trained physicians means that over-the-counter antibiotic purchases without proper diagnosis are extremely common, directly accelerating resistance.

Key Facts

  • By 2050, drug-resistant infections could kill 10 million people annually — more than cancer.
  • At least 700,000 people worldwide already die each year from resistant infections.
  • Over 70% of antibiotics sold globally are used in animal agriculture, not human medicine.
  • India is one of the largest consumers of antibiotics per capita in the world.

The Local Impact

In rural and semi-urban areas like Silchar and Karimganj, the practice of buying antibiotics directly from pharmacies without a doctor's prescription is widespread. Common conditions like fever, cough, and diarrhea are routinely treated with broad-spectrum antibiotics, even when the underlying cause is viral — where antibiotics have absolutely zero effect. This habitual misuse is creating a reservoir of resistant bacteria in our communities.

What You Can Do

  • Never self-medicate: Always consult a qualified physician before taking any antibiotic. A proper diagnosis determines whether your infection is bacterial or viral.
  • Complete the full course: Even if you feel better after two days, stopping antibiotics early allows partially-weakened bacteria to survive and develop resistance.
  • Refuse unnecessary prescriptions: If a doctor prescribes antibiotics for a common cold (which is viral), politely ask whether the prescription is truly necessary.
  • Practice hygiene: Regular handwashing with soap, safe food preparation, and clean drinking water prevent infections in the first place — reducing the need for antibiotics entirely.
  • Spread awareness: Educate your family, especially elderly relatives who may be accustomed to buying antibiotics over the counter, about the dangers of misuse.
