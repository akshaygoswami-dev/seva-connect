Stunting & Parasites: The Silent Threat to Our Children

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Across rural Assam, millions of children are trapped in a devastating cycle: intestinal parasites steal the very nutrients growing bodies need, leading to chronic malnutrition and irreversible stunted growth. The Barak Valley — with its high rates of open defecation, limited sanitation infrastructure, and contaminated water sources — is a hotspot for this silent epidemic that robs children of their future potential.

The Parasite–Malnutrition Cycle

Soil-transmitted helminths — primarily roundworm (Ascaris lumbricoides), hookworm (Ancylostoma duodenale), and whipworm (Trichuris trichiura) — infect over 1.5 billion people worldwide, with the highest burden among children aged 1–14 in tropical developing regions. Children become infected when they ingest parasite eggs from contaminated soil, water, or unwashed vegetables, or when hookworm larvae penetrate bare feet walking on infected ground.

Once inside the body, these worms colonise the intestines and feed on blood, tissue, and ingested nutrients. Hookworms alone can cause the loss of up to 0.25 mL of blood per worm per day — a child harbouring 200 hookworms loses enough blood to develop severe iron-deficiency anaemia. The resulting malnutrition impairs cognitive development, weakens immunity, and causes chronic stunting — a condition where a child's height-for-age falls more than two standard deviations below the WHO median. Once a child is stunted by age 2, the damage to brain development and physical growth is largely irreversible.

Key Facts

  • India accounts for approximately 25% of the world's stunted children — over 40 million under-fives are affected nationally.
  • In Assam, the NFHS-5 survey found that 35.3% of children under five are stunted, with rural districts in the Barak Valley reporting rates above 40%.
  • WHO recommends mass deworming of all school-age children in endemic areas at least once a year; a single dose of albendazole costs less than ₹5.
  • Studies show that dewormed children gain an average of 0.24 kg more body weight and show measurable improvements in school attendance and concentration.

Local Initiatives & Gaps

The Integrated Child Development Services (ICDS) programme operates Anganwadi centres across Cachar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi districts, providing supplementary nutrition, growth monitoring, and periodic deworming to children under six. India's National Deworming Day — held biannually in February and August — aims to deworm all children aged 1–19 through schools and Anganwadis. However, coverage in the Barak Valley remains uneven: remote tea garden communities and char (river island) populations are often missed, and many parents remain unaware that a single pill can transform their child's health trajectory.

What You Can Do

  • Ensure biannual deworming: Take your children to the nearest Anganwadi centre or school on National Deworming Day. Albendazole is provided free of charge and is safe for children over 1 year old.
  • Build and use latrines: Open defecation is the primary source of soil contamination with parasite eggs. Use available government subsidies under the Swachh Bharat Mission to construct household toilets.
  • Practice handwashing with soap: Wash hands thoroughly before eating and after using the toilet. Teach children this habit as early as possible — it is the single most effective barrier against ingesting parasite eggs.
  • Wear footwear outdoors: Hookworm larvae penetrate bare skin. Ensuring children wear sandals or shoes when playing outside breaks a critical transmission route.
  • Wash produce thoroughly: Rinse all fruits and vegetables under clean running water before consumption, especially those grown in or near the ground.
