Cyber Security: Staying Safe in the Digital World

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

As India races toward a digital-first economy, cybercriminals are evolving just as fast. From UPI fraud and phishing emails to deepfake videos and social media impersonation, the threats are real, sophisticated, and increasingly targeting people in smaller towns and rural areas who are newer to digital platforms.

The Growing Digital Threat Landscape

India reported over 65,000 cyber crime cases in 2022 — a number that has more than tripled in five years. The Indian Cyber Crime Coordination Centre (I4C) estimates that Indians lost over ₹10,319 crore to online fraud in 2024 alone. The most common attacks are devastatingly simple: a fake SMS claiming your bank account will be blocked, a WhatsApp message offering a "government subsidy," or a phone call from someone posing as a customer care executive asking for your OTP.

In Assam and the Barak Valley, the rapid adoption of UPI-based payments through PhonePe, Google Pay, and Paytm — combined with limited digital literacy among first-time smartphone users — has created a fertile ground for scammers. Elderly residents and small shopkeepers in Silchar are frequent targets of "collect request" scams, where fraudsters send UPI collect requests disguised as refunds or prize winnings.

Key Facts

  • 92% of phishing attacks begin with an email, SMS, or WhatsApp message — never click links from unknown senders.
  • No bank, UPI app, or government agency will ever ask for your OTP, PIN, or CVV over phone or text.
  • AI-generated deepfake videos and voice clones are now being used to impersonate family members requesting emergency money transfers.
  • The National Cyber Crime Reporting Portal (cybercrime.gov.in) and helpline 1930 allow you to report fraud within the critical first few hours.

Password Hygiene & Social Media Privacy

Weak passwords remain the single largest vulnerability. Using "123456," your date of birth, or the same password across every account is an open invitation to hackers. Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on every account that offers it — especially email, banking, and social media. On platforms like Facebook and Instagram, review your privacy settings regularly: restrict who can see your posts, friend list, and personal information. Scammers routinely mine public profiles to craft convincing phishing messages personalized with your name, workplace, and photos.

What You Can Do

  • Verify before you trust: If you receive a call or message claiming to be from your bank, hang up and call the official customer care number directly. Never share OTPs, PINs, or passwords with anyone — not even someone claiming to be a bank manager.
  • Use strong, unique passwords: Create passwords with at least 12 characters combining uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols. Use a password manager to keep track of them securely.
  • Beware of "too good to be true" offers: Lottery winnings, job offers requiring upfront payments, and WhatsApp investment groups promising 50% monthly returns are almost always scams.
  • Keep devices updated: Software updates patch security vulnerabilities. Enable automatic updates on your smartphone, laptop, and apps.
  • Educate your family: Sit with elderly family members and walk them through common scam patterns. Teaching them to say "I will call you back" before acting on any request can prevent most fraud.
