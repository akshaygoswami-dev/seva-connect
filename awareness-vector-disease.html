Vector-Borne Diseases: Fighting Back Against Mosquitoes

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

The Barak Valley's warm, humid climate and abundant monsoon rainfall create a perfect storm for mosquito-borne diseases. Dengue, malaria, Japanese encephalitis (JE), and chikungunya collectively account for thousands of hospitalisations across Cachar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi districts each year — yet the majority of these infections are preventable with community awareness and basic environmental management.

The Diseases We Face

Dengue, transmitted by the day-biting Aedes aegypti mosquito, causes high fever, severe body aches, and in its haemorrhagic form, a dangerous drop in platelet count that can lead to internal bleeding and death. Every monsoon season, Silchar Medical College Hospital reports a surge of dengue patients, with families scrambling for platelet transfusions as blood banks run critically low.

Malaria, caused by Plasmodium parasites transmitted through Anopheles mosquitoes that bite primarily at dusk and night, remains endemic in the Barak Valley's forested and tea garden areas. Japanese encephalitis — transmitted by Culex mosquitoes breeding in rice paddies — causes brain inflammation with a 20–30% case fatality rate and permanent neurological damage in up to 50% of survivors. Chikungunya produces debilitating joint pain that can persist for months or even years after the initial fever resolves.

Key Facts

  • India reports over 100,000 dengue cases annually, with Assam consistently ranking among the top ten affected states during the post-monsoon peak.
  • A single Aedes aegypti female can lay up to 500 eggs in her lifetime — a single discarded bottle cap holding stagnant water is sufficient breeding ground.
  • Long-lasting insecticidal nets (LLINs) reduce malaria transmission by up to 50% and are distributed free under the National Vector Borne Disease Control Programme.
  • Japanese encephalitis vaccination is included in India's Universal Immunization Programme for children in endemic districts, but coverage in the Barak Valley remains below target.

Why the Barak Valley Is Vulnerable

The region's geography works against us. Extensive rice paddy cultivation provides ideal breeding habitat for Culex and Anopheles mosquitoes, while rapid urbanisation in Silchar without adequate drainage systems means stagnant water pools persist in construction sites, rooftop tanks, discarded tyres, and blocked storm drains throughout the city. During the annual monsoon floods — a near-certainty in the Barak Valley — displaced water creates vast temporary breeding grounds, and the subsequent dengue and malaria surges overwhelm the district's limited hospital infrastructure.

What You Can Do

  • Eliminate standing water weekly: Inspect your home and surroundings every Sunday. Empty flower pots, old tyres, coconut shells, water cooler trays, and any container that holds stagnant water. It takes just 7 days for mosquito eggs to become biting adults.
  • Sleep under bed nets: Use long-lasting insecticidal nets (LLINs), especially for children and pregnant women. These are available free from your nearest Primary Health Centre under the NVBDCP programme.
  • Use repellents and protective clothing: Apply mosquito repellent on exposed skin during dawn and dusk. Wear light-coloured, long-sleeved clothing — mosquitoes are attracted to dark colours.
  • Get children vaccinated for JE: Ensure your child receives the Japanese encephalitis vaccine as part of their routine immunisation schedule. Contact your nearest Anganwadi or PHC if doses have been missed.
  • Seek early diagnosis: If you develop sudden high fever with body ache, do not self-medicate with aspirin or ibuprofen (which worsen dengue bleeding). Visit a healthcare facility immediately for a dengue NS1 or malaria rapid diagnostic test.
