Zero Dose Children: The Vaccine Gap Crisis

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

A "zero-dose" child is one who has never received a single dose of any routine vaccine — not even the most basic DPT-1 shot. According to UNICEF and Gavi, approximately 14.3 million children worldwide were zero-dose in 2023, meaning they remain completely unprotected against preventable diseases like measles, polio, diphtheria, and whooping cough. India alone accounts for over 2.7 million of these children, the highest national figure on the planet.

Why Children Fall Through the Cracks

Zero-dose children are not randomly distributed — they are concentrated in the most marginalized communities. Families living in remote rural areas, urban slums, conflict zones, and migrant settlements face enormous barriers to accessing immunization services. In many cases, the nearest health sub-centre may be hours away by foot. Poverty forces parents to choose between a day's wages and a clinic visit. Misinformation and vaccine hesitancy, amplified by social media, further erode trust in immunization programmes.

In Assam's Barak Valley, geographic isolation compounds the problem. Char (riverine island) communities along the Barak River, tea garden populations, and families in flood-prone areas of Cachar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi districts frequently miss routine immunization sessions. Seasonal flooding disrupts cold chain infrastructure, rendering vaccines unusable. Health workers struggle to maintain updated beneficiary lists in areas with high internal migration, meaning many newborns are never registered for vaccination at all.

Key Facts

  • 14.3 million children globally received zero routine vaccines in 2023 — a number that rose sharply during the COVID-19 pandemic.
  • India's Intensified Mission Indradhanush (IMI) 5.0 specifically targets zero-dose and under-vaccinated children in 313 districts.
  • Measles alone killed an estimated 136,000 children worldwide in 2022, mostly those who were unvaccinated.
  • Assam's full immunization coverage stands at approximately 67% — well below the national target of 90%.

The Local Impact

In Silchar and surrounding areas, Seva Connect volunteers have observed that awareness about the importance of completing the full immunization schedule remains alarmingly low among daily-wage families. Many parents are unaware that the government provides all childhood vaccines free of cost under the Universal Immunization Programme (UIP). Language barriers in multilingual communities and distrust of outsiders further complicate outreach in hard-to-reach pockets of Barak Valley.

What You Can Do

  • Ensure complete vaccination: Check your child's immunization card and ensure every scheduled dose — from birth to age 5 — has been administered. The full schedule includes BCG, OPV, Hepatitis B, Pentavalent, Rotavirus, PCV, IPV, MR, JE, and DPT boosters.
  • Spread the word: Inform neighbours and relatives about upcoming immunization sessions at local Anganwadi centres and Primary Health Centres. Many families simply don't know when and where vaccines are available.
  • Support ASHA workers: Accredited Social Health Activists (ASHAs) are the frontline soldiers of immunization. Support their door-to-door campaigns by cooperating during household surveys and mobilizing your community.
  • Counter misinformation: If you encounter vaccine myths on WhatsApp or social media, share factual information from the WHO, UNICEF, or the Ministry of Health and Family Welfare.
  • Volunteer with outreach drives: Join Seva Connect's health awareness campaigns to help identify zero-dose children in your locality and connect their families with immunization services.

Take Action

Every child deserves protection from preventable diseases. Join Seva Connect's immunization awareness drives and help ensure no child in Barak Valley is left behind.
