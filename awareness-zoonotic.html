Zoonotic Diseases: When Animal Illnesses Jump to Humans

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Zoonotic diseases — infections that jump from animals to humans — are responsible for some of humanity's deadliest pandemics, from rabies and avian influenza to the SARS-CoV-2 virus that caused COVID-19. In the Barak Valley, where daily human-animal contact is a way of life, understanding zoonotic transmission is not an academic exercise — it is a matter of community survival.

How Zoonotic Diseases Spread

Pathogens cross the species barrier through multiple pathways: direct contact with infected animal saliva, blood, urine, or faeces; consumption of undercooked meat or unpasteurised milk; bites and scratches from infected animals; and indirect transmission via insect vectors that feed on both animals and humans. The risk escalates wherever humans live in close proximity to livestock, poultry, and stray animals — precisely the conditions found across rural Barak Valley households, where cattle share compounds with families, backyard poultry roam freely, and stray dog populations remain largely unvaccinated.

Globally, approximately 75% of all emerging infectious diseases in humans are zoonotic in origin. Wildlife trade and habitat destruction — including deforestation in Assam's biodiversity hotspots — force wild animals into closer contact with human settlements, creating new opportunities for viruses to jump species. The devastating lessons of COVID-19, which is believed to have originated in a wildlife-human interface, underscore how a local zoonotic spillover event can escalate into a global catastrophe within weeks.

Key Facts

  • India reports approximately 20,000 rabies deaths annually — the highest in the world — with most cases caused by stray dog bites in rural areas.
  • Avian influenza (H5N1) outbreaks have been detected in poultry farms across Assam multiple times since 2008, requiring mass culling of infected birds.
  • The "One Health" approach — coordinating human medicine, veterinary science, and environmental health — is endorsed by WHO, FAO, and OIE as essential for preventing zoonotic pandemics.
  • Post-exposure rabies prophylaxis (PEP) is 100% effective when administered promptly after a bite, but delayed treatment is almost always fatal once symptoms appear.

Rabies: The Barak Valley's Persistent Killer

Rabies remains the most immediate zoonotic threat in our region. Cachar district has a large population of unvaccinated stray dogs, and bites — particularly among children who play in streets and open areas — are alarmingly common. Despite the availability of free post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) at district hospitals, many bite victims in remote areas either delay seeking treatment due to distance, or rely on traditional remedies that have zero medical efficacy. Once rabies symptoms manifest — hydrophobia, aggression, paralysis — the disease is virtually 100% fatal. Every dog bite must be treated as a medical emergency: thorough wound washing with soap and running water for 15 minutes, followed by immediate hospital visit for the rabies vaccine series.

What You Can Do

  • Vaccinate your pets and livestock: Ensure all dogs, cats, and cattle in your household receive their scheduled vaccinations. Support community animal vaccination drives organised by the district veterinary department.
  • Treat every animal bite as urgent: Wash the wound immediately with soap and running water for at least 15 minutes, then go directly to the nearest hospital for post-exposure rabies prophylaxis. Do not apply turmeric, chilli, or herbal pastes — these delay proper treatment and can cause infection.
  • Practice safe food handling: Cook all meat and eggs thoroughly to an internal temperature that kills pathogens. Avoid consuming raw or undercooked poultry, and never handle sick or dead birds with bare hands.
  • Avoid contact with wild animals: Do not touch, feed, or attempt to rescue sick-looking bats, monkeys, or wild birds. Report unusual animal die-offs to the local veterinary officer or forest department immediately.
  • Support the One Health approach: Advocate for coordinated action between human health workers, veterinarians, and environmental authorities in your panchayat. Zoonotic disease prevention requires all three sectors working together.
