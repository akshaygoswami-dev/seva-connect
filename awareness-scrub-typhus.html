Scrub Typhus: The Hidden Threat in Our Fields

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Scrub typhus is an acute febrile illness caused by the bacterium Orientia tsutsugamushi, transmitted to humans through the bite of infected trombiculid mite larvae — commonly known as chiggers. Found lurking in scrubland, rice paddies, and forest clearings across the Barak Valley, this disease remains one of the most underdiagnosed yet dangerous infections in rural Assam.

How It Spreads

Trombiculid mites thrive in warm, humid environments with dense vegetation — conditions found throughout southern Assam's agricultural landscape. The larval stage of these mites, barely visible to the naked eye, attaches to skin while people work in fields, collect firewood, or walk through tall grass. The bite is usually painless, and the mite feeds for several days before dropping off, often leaving behind a characteristic dark, painless scab called an eschar — the hallmark sign of scrub typhus.

Within 6 to 21 days of the bite, patients develop high fever, severe headache, muscle pain, and swollen lymph nodes. A distinctive maculopapular rash may appear on the trunk before spreading to the limbs. Without prompt treatment using doxycycline or azithromycin, the disease can progress to multi-organ failure, pneumonia, meningoencephalitis, and death — with untreated case fatality rates ranging from 6% to over 35%.

Key Facts

  • Scrub typhus accounts for up to 25% of all undifferentiated febrile illnesses in parts of rural India.
  • Assam's National Health Mission has documented recurring outbreaks across the Barak Valley, particularly during and after the monsoon season (July–November).
  • The eschar — a painless black scab at the bite site — is present in only 7–68% of Indian patients, making clinical diagnosis challenging.
  • Early treatment with doxycycline reduces the case fatality rate to below 1%, but delayed diagnosis remains the primary cause of death.

The Barak Valley Context

In the districts of Cachar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi, rice cultivation and tea garden work bring thousands of labourers into daily contact with mite-infested vegetation. Post-monsoon outbreaks frequently overwhelm Silchar Medical College Hospital, where scrub typhus often mimics dengue or malaria in its early stages, leading to dangerous misdiagnosis. A 2019 seroprevalence study in Assam found that over 30% of tested febrile patients in endemic zones carried antibodies against O. tsutsugamushi, indicating widespread but underreported transmission.

What You Can Do

  • Use DEET-based repellents: Apply insect repellent containing 20–30% DEET on exposed skin and clothing before entering fields, forested areas, or scrubland. Reapply every few hours for continued protection.
  • Wear protective clothing: Long sleeves, full-length trousers tucked into socks, and closed shoes significantly reduce mite access to your skin during outdoor work.
  • Check for eschars: After working in fields, inspect your body — especially skin folds, waistbands, and ankles — for tiny bites or the telltale dark scab. An eschar combined with fever should prompt an immediate hospital visit.
  • Seek early diagnosis: If you develop unexplained fever lasting more than 5 days, especially during or after the monsoon, insist on scrub typhus testing (IgM ELISA or rapid diagnostic kits). Early doxycycline treatment is curative.
  • Clear vegetation around homes: Keep grass trimmed short, remove leaf litter, and avoid drying clothes on bushes — all of which reduce mite habitats near living areas.
