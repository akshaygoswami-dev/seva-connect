Women's Safety: Awareness, Rights & Empowerment

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Women's safety is not merely a law-and-order issue — it is a measure of how civilised, equitable, and progressive a society truly is. Despite decades of legislation, India recorded over 4.45 lakh cases of crimes against women in 2022. Real safety demands more than policing; it requires awareness of rights, community vigilance, and a cultural shift that treats women as equal stakeholders in public and private life.

Know Your Legal Rights

Indian law provides robust protections for women, yet a significant majority remain unaware of the legal tools at their disposal. The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act (2005) covers physical, emotional, verbal, and economic abuse. The Sexual Harassment of Women at Workplace Act (2013) mandates every organisation with ten or more employees to constitute an Internal Complaints Committee (ICC). Section 354 of the Indian Penal Code criminalises assault or use of criminal force intended to outrage a woman's modesty. Knowledge of these rights is the first line of defence.

In the Barak Valley, many women — particularly in rural and semi-urban areas around Karimganj, Hailakandi, and Silchar — are unaware that they can file complaints anonymously, that police are legally obligated to register an FIR regardless of jurisdiction, and that free legal aid is available through the District Legal Services Authority. Local self-help groups (SHGs) and NGOs are increasingly bridging this awareness gap, but much more ground remains to be covered.

Key Facts

  • The Women Helpline number 181 is available 24/7, toll-free, across all states including Assam.
  • Under Indian law, a woman can file a Zero FIR at any police station — there is no jurisdictional bar.
  • The Nirbhaya Fund has allocated over ₹12,000 crore for women's safety infrastructure and schemes since 2013.
  • Assam's "Sakhi" One Stop Centres provide medical, legal, and psychological support to women in distress.

What You Can Do

  • Save emergency numbers: Keep Women Helpline 181, Police 112, and the local One Stop Centre number saved on your phone and share them with every woman in your family and circle.
  • Learn basic self-defence: Self-defence awareness is not about physical strength — it is about situational awareness, de-escalation, and knowing how to create distance and call for help. Community self-defence workshops are a powerful tool for confidence.
  • Commute safely: Share live location with a trusted contact during late-night travel, avoid isolated routes, and use verified ride-hailing services. If using public transport, note the vehicle registration number and share it.
  • Demand workplace accountability: Every woman has the right to a harassment-free workplace. If your employer lacks an ICC, report the non-compliance to the District Officer under the POSH Act.
  • Be an active bystander: If you witness harassment — on the street, in public transport, or online — speak up, intervene safely, or help the victim report the incident. Silence enables perpetrators.
