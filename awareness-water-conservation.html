Water Conservation: Protecting Our Most Precious Resource

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Water covers 71% of our planet, yet only 2.5% of it is freshwater — and less than 1% is accessible for human use. India, home to 18% of the world's population, possesses only 4% of global freshwater resources. With groundwater levels plummeting and rivers increasingly polluted, water conservation is no longer optional. It is an existential priority.

The Crisis Beneath Our Feet

India is the largest groundwater consumer on Earth, extracting over 250 cubic kilometres annually — more than the United States and China combined. The Central Ground Water Board has classified numerous blocks across Assam as "semi-critical" or "over-exploited." In the Barak Valley, the rapid expansion of Silchar's urban footprint, combined with unchecked borewell drilling and the disappearance of natural wetlands (beels), has accelerated water table depletion at an alarming rate.

Meanwhile, the Barak River and its tributaries — once lifelines for drinking water, irrigation, and fisheries — face mounting pollution from untreated sewage, agricultural runoff laden with pesticides, and industrial discharge. During the dry months of November through March, many communities in Cachar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi districts experience acute water scarcity, relying on tanker supplies at inflated costs. The paradox is striking: a region that experiences devastating floods during monsoon suffers water shortages just months later, because almost none of that rainwater is captured or stored.

Key Facts

  • By 2030, India's water demand is projected to be twice the available supply, affecting hundreds of millions.
  • Agriculture consumes nearly 80% of India's freshwater — much of it wasted through flood irrigation methods.
  • A single leaking tap can waste over 11,000 litres of water per year.
  • Rainwater harvesting from a 100 sq. metre rooftop in Silchar (avg. rainfall ~3,000 mm) can collect approximately 2.4 lakh litres annually.

What You Can Do

  • Fix leaks immediately: A dripping tap or a running toilet cistern wastes thousands of litres silently. Regular plumbing maintenance is one of the simplest and most impactful conservation steps.
  • Install rainwater harvesting: The Barak Valley receives exceptionally heavy rainfall. Even a basic rooftop collection system with a storage tank can supplement household water needs for months and recharge groundwater.
  • Use water wisely at home: Turn off the tap while brushing teeth, use a bucket instead of a running hose for washing vehicles, and run washing machines only with full loads. These habits can reduce household consumption by 30–40%.
  • Protect local water bodies: Never dispose of waste, chemicals, or sewage into rivers, ponds, or drains. Participate in community clean-up drives for local beels and river ghats. Healthy water bodies recharge the groundwater we depend on.
  • Advocate for better practices: Encourage farmers in your network to adopt drip irrigation or sprinkler systems instead of flood irrigation. Support local panchayats in implementing Jal Jeevan Mission infrastructure for piped water and water recycling.
