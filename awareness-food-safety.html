Food Safety: Protecting What You Eat

4 min read • Updated: June 2026

Food is meant to nourish, but contaminated and adulterated food silently harms millions of Indians every year. From synthetic colors in sweets to pesticide-laden vegetables, the threats to food safety are pervasive — and often invisible to the naked eye. In the Barak Valley, where open-air markets and street food are integral to daily life, awareness of what goes into your plate is more important than ever.

The Hidden Dangers in Our Food

The World Health Organization estimates that unsafe food causes 600 million cases of foodborne illness globally each year, resulting in 4.2 lakh deaths. In India, food adulteration is a widespread problem: the Food Safety and Standards Authority of India (FSSAI) found that nearly 28% of food samples tested across the country in recent surveys failed to meet safety standards. Common adulterants include metanil yellow in turmeric, chalk powder in flour, water and starch in milk, urea in sweets, and formaldehyde in fish to extend shelf life.

In the Barak Valley, the humid subtropical climate accelerates food spoilage, making proper storage and handling critical. Markets in Silchar, Karimganj, and Hailakandi operate largely in open-air settings with limited cold storage infrastructure. Fish — a dietary staple across the region — is particularly vulnerable to bacterial contamination when transported without refrigeration from distant ponds and rivers. During the monsoon season, the risk of waterborne contamination of food supplies increases dramatically as floodwaters mix with sewage and agricultural runoff.

Key Facts

  • Nearly 28% of food samples tested by FSSAI across India were found to be adulterated or substandard.
  • Foodborne diseases cause an estimated 4.2 lakh deaths worldwide every year — children under 5 are the most affected.
  • The FSSAI license number on packaged food guarantees that the product has been manufactured under regulated hygiene conditions.
  • Simple home tests — like rubbing turmeric on paper to check for metanil yellow, or adding iodine to milk to detect starch — can help detect common adulterants.

Street Food & Market Hygiene

Street food is an affordable, beloved part of food culture across the Barak Valley — from puchka and jhalmuri to singara and chop. However, roadside vendors often lack access to clean water, proper waste disposal, and handwashing facilities. Reuse of cooking oil until it darkens and smokes, unrefrigerated storage of perishable ingredients, and exposure of food to dust and vehicle emissions are common concerns. The FSSAI's "Clean Street Food Hub" initiative aims to train and certify street food vendors in basic hygiene practices — a program that deserves wider adoption in towns across Cachar.

What You Can Do

  • Buy smart: Always check for FSSAI license numbers on packaged food. Purchase perishables like fish, meat, and dairy from vendors who use proper refrigeration or ice storage.
  • Store food safely: Keep cooked food refrigerated within two hours of preparation. Never refreeze thawed food. Store grains and pulses in airtight containers to prevent pest contamination and fungal growth.
  • Wash thoroughly: Wash all fruits and vegetables under running water before consumption, even if you plan to peel them. Soak leafy greens in salt water or a dilute vinegar solution for 10 minutes to remove pesticide residues.
  • Practice kitchen hygiene: Wash hands with soap before handling food. Use separate cutting boards for raw meat and vegetables. Ensure drinking water is filtered or boiled, especially during the monsoon season.
  • Report adulteration: If you suspect food adulteration, report it through the FSSAI's online Food Safety Connect portal or call the toll-free food safety helpline at 1800-112-100.
