Basic First Aid: Saving Lives Before Help Arrives

6 min read • Updated: June 2026

In a medical emergency, the first few minutes are absolutely critical. Often, professional ambulances and paramedics can take 15 to 30 minutes to arrive on the scene. During this gap, the actions taken by bystandersâ€”ordinary people with basic first aid knowledgeâ€”are what determine whether a victim survives.

The Golden Rule of First Aid: DRABC

If you witness an accident or find someone collapsed, never panic. Follow the globally recognized DRABC action plan:

  • D - Danger: Before rushing in, check your surroundings. Is there live electricity, oncoming traffic, or fire? You cannot help the victim if you become a victim yourself.
  • R - Response: Check if the person is conscious. Ask loudly, "Can you hear me?" and gently tap their shoulders.
  • A - Airway: If they are unresponsive, gently tilt their head back and lift their chin to open the airway. Ensure nothing is blocking their mouth.
  • B - Breathing: Look, listen, and feel for normal breathing for up to 10 seconds. If they are not breathing normally, you must begin CPR immediately.
  • C - Circulation: If they are breathing, look for severe bleeding and apply direct pressure to any major wounds.

How to Perform Hands-Only CPR

If an adult is completely unresponsive and not breathing normally:

  • Call emergency services immediately.
  • Place the heel of one hand in the center of their chest, and place your other hand on top, interlocking your fingers.
  • Keep your arms straight and push hard and fastâ€”at least 2 inches deep, at a rate of 100 to 120 compressions per minute (to the beat of the song "Stayin' Alive").
  • Do not stop until medical professionals arrive or the person wakes up.

Handling Common Emergencies

Severe Bleeding

The priority is to stop the blood loss. Apply firm, direct pressure over the wound using a clean cloth, towel, or your hands if necessary. If the blood soaks through, do not remove the cloth; just place another one on top and keep pressing. If the wound is on a limb, elevate it above the level of the heart to slow the blood flow.

Burns

For minor to moderate burns (like from boiling water or hot surfaces), immediately hold the burned area under cool (not ice-cold) running water for at least 20 minutes. Do not apply ice, butter, or toothpaste, as these can trap heat and worsen the damage. Cover the burn loosely with a sterile, non-stick dressing or cling film.

Choking

If a person is clutching their throat and cannot speak or cough, they are choking. Perform the Heimlich maneuver: stand behind them, wrap your arms around their waist, make a fist just above their navel, grab your fist with your other hand, and give 5 quick, upward thrusts. Alternate with 5 back blows between the shoulder blades until the object is dislodged.

Build Your Emergency Kit

Every home and car should have a basic first aid kit containing:

  • Sterile gauze pads of various sizes and adhesive bandages (band-aids)
  • Antiseptic wipes and antibiotic ointment
  • A digital thermometer and medical tweezers
  • Pain relievers (Paracetamol/Ibuprofen)
  • A pair of disposable latex or nitrile gloves
